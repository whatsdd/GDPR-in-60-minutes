# Glossary

The GDPR uses about 25 specific terms. The official definitions live in <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2050-1-1" label="Article 4 GDPR" /> and a handful of other articles. Below is the plain-English version. The exact legal wording is one click away on the EUR-Lex badge.

## The core five

### Personal data

Any information that points to a living person, directly or indirectly. A name is personal data. So is an email, a phone number, a customer ID, a photo, a location ping, an IP address, a cookie ID. If you could use it (alone or combined with other info you already have) to figure out who somebody is, it counts. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2050-1-1" label="Art. 4(1)" />

### Processing

Anything you do with personal data. Collecting it, storing it, looking at it, copying it, sending it, deleting it. Even just keeping a CSV on a laptop is "processing." <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2050-1-1" label="Art. 4(2)" />

### Controller

The person or company that decides **why** to collect the data and **how** to use it. If you run an online shop, you are the controller of your customers' data. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2050-1-1" label="Art. 4(7)" />

### Processor

A company you hire that handles the data on your behalf and only does what you tell it to. Your email tool, your cloud host, your payroll provider. They are processors. You are still the controller. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2050-1-1" label="Art. 4(8)" />

### Data subject

The person whose info is being handled. We mostly just say "the person" or "your customer" on this site. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2050-1-1" label="Art. 4(1)" />

## Words you will see a lot

### Consent

A clear, informed yes from the person. It must be a real choice. A pre-ticked box does not count. Silence does not count. You have to be able to prove they said yes, and they can change their mind any time. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2050-1-1" label="Art. 4(11)" />

### Lawful basis

The reason in the law that lets you keep this info at all. There are six (see [Module 4](/modules/04-lawful-bases)). You need exactly one for each thing you do. Consent is just one of the six. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2178-1-1" label="Art. 6" />

### Special category data

The extra-sensitive stuff: health, race, religion, sexuality, political views, trade union membership, genetics, biometrics. The rules for handling it are tougher. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2294-1-1" label="Art. 9" />

### Personal data breach

When the info you hold gets lost, stolen, leaked, or accidentally changed. Even an email sent to the wrong person can count. See [Module 7](/modules/07-security-breach) for what to do. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2050-1-1" label="Art. 4(12)" />

### Pseudonymisation

You scramble the names but keep a key somewhere to unscramble them. Lowers the risk but the data still counts as personal data. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2050-1-1" label="Art. 4(5)" />

### Anonymisation

You strip the data so nobody, including you, can ever link it back to a person. Truly anonymised data falls outside the GDPR. Most "anonymised" data is actually just pseudonymised, so be careful. (See Recital 26 in the GDPR for the official line.)

### Profiling

Using software to make decisions about a person automatically. For example, an algorithm deciding whether you get a loan. Extra rules apply (see [Module 5](/modules/05-data-subject-rights)). <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2050-1-1" label="Art. 4(4)" />

## The official people in the system

### Data Protection Officer (DPO)

An internal point of contact for privacy questions. Some companies must appoint one (see [Module 9](/modules/09-dpo-dpia)). Many do it voluntarily. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e3922-1-1" label="Art. 37" />

### Supervisory authority

The regulator in each EU country (and the UK has its own, the ICO). They take complaints, run investigations, and issue fines. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2050-1-1" label="Art. 4(21)" />

### European Data Protection Board (EDPB)

A board that brings all the EU regulators together and publishes guidelines so the rules are applied the same way everywhere. You will see references like "EDPB Guidelines 05/2020." <ArticleRef href="https://www.edpb.europa.eu/" label="edpb.europa.eu" />

## Words that look the same but are not

### Privacy notice vs. privacy policy

Notice = the short, clear thing you show your users (often called a "privacy policy" online). Policy = the internal document inside your company that says how you handle this stuff. The law focuses on the notice. <ArticleRef href="https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679#d1e2870-1-1" label="Art. 13" />

### Cookie consent vs. GDPR consent

Cookies are governed by a different rule (the ePrivacy Directive) which sits on top of the GDPR. Most cookies need a yes-tick before they load. The GDPR then governs what you do with whatever the cookies collect.
