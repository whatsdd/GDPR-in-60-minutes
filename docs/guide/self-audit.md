# Quick Self-Audit

If you can tick most of the boxes below, you are in decent shape. Each section maps to one of the ten modules, so when you find a gap, you know where to read.

## The basics (Module 1 and 2)

- [ ] I can name the people whose info we keep (customers, users, employees, leads, suppliers).
- [ ] I know whether any of those people live in Europe.
- [ ] Somebody in the company can say in one sentence what data we collect and why.

## How we tell people what we do (Module 3 and 5)

- [ ] We have a privacy notice on our website.
- [ ] The notice is written in plain language and links to the actual choices people can make.
- [ ] If somebody emails us asking "what data do you have on me," we know who handles it and roughly how long it should take.

## The real reason we keep data (Module 4)

- [ ] For each thing we do with people's info (sign-ups, marketing emails, analytics, support tickets), we know which of the six lawful bases we are using.
- [ ] If we rely on consent for anything, we can show a record of who said yes and when.
- [ ] We delete info we do not need any more, on a schedule.

## Vendors and tools (Module 6)

- [ ] We have a list of every company that touches our customer data (email tool, analytics, cloud host, CRM, payments, support, etc.).
- [ ] We have a written contract with each one that covers data protection (a "Data Processing Agreement" or DPA).
- [ ] We know which of those vendors store the data outside Europe.

## Security and what to do if it goes wrong (Module 7)

- [ ] We can name our top three security risks.
- [ ] We use strong passwords, two-factor authentication, and at least basic encryption on devices.
- [ ] We have a one-page plan for what to do if customer data leaks, gets stolen, or goes to the wrong person.
- [ ] At least one person knows we have 72 hours to tell the regulator if a serious breach happens.

## Sending data abroad (Module 8)

- [ ] We know which of our tools send data to the US or other non-EU countries.
- [ ] For each, we know whether the country has an "adequacy decision" or we are using Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs).

## DPO and big projects (Module 9)

- [ ] We have thought about whether we need a Data Protection Officer (most small businesses do not, but some do).
- [ ] Before launching anything that uses sensitive info or new tech (AI, biometrics, large-scale tracking), we run a quick risk check.

## If something goes wrong (Module 10)

- [ ] We have written down the date we last reviewed our privacy setup.
- [ ] We know which national regulator would be ours if a complaint came in.

## How did you do?

- **17 or more ticked**: You have the spine of a working privacy programme. Keep going.
- **10 to 16**: Good start. Pick the section with the most blanks and read the matching module.
- **Under 10**: This is normal for an early-stage business. Start with Module 1 and Module 4 (lawful bases). They will cover most of what is missing.
